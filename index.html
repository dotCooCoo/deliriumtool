    CAM-ICU Screening
    Perform once per shift and with any acute change in mental status
    1
    Acute Onset & Fluctuating Course Required

    Evidence of acute change from mental status baseline, OR fluctuation during the day? Anchor "baseline" with family/collateral on admission — Feature 1 cannot be scored without it.

    2
    Inattention Required

    Letters task: say S-A-V-E-A-H-A-A-R-T (10 letters, ~1 every 3 sec); patient squeezes on each "A"

    Can't do letters (deaf, language barrier, non-verbal)? Use the Pictures ASE (5 picture cards) instead — same >2-error cutoff.

    3
    Altered Level of Consciousness
    4
    Disorganized Thinking

    Ask the 4 yes/no questions, then the command. Disorganized thinking present if combined errors >1.

    1. Will a stone float on water?
    2. Are there fish in the sea?
    3. Does one pound weigh more than two pounds?
    4. Can you use a hammer to pound a nail?

    Command: "Hold up this many fingers" (examiner holds up 2), then "Now do the same with the other hand" (or add one finger). Any wrong answer or failed command counts as one error.

    CAM Result
    —
    Delirium Status
    Incomplete
    Complete Features 1 & 2 + one of 3 or 4
    F1 Acute/fluctuating F2 Inattention 3 or 4 LOC / thinking
    Positive if 1 AND 2 AND (3 OR 4)

    Limitations: score against the patient's baseline; reduced accuracy in primary neurologic injury/TBI, aphasia, or deep sedation. Missed cases are predominantly hypoactive — screen every shift.

    A positive CAM-ICU is a screen, not a diagnosis — confirm clinically and exclude mimics.

    Delirium vs mimics
    Dementia — chronic, stable; delirium is acute, fluctuating, with inattention.
    Depression — can look hypoactive; mood-driven, attention less affected.
    Receptive aphasia / focal stroke — focal deficit, not global inattention → neuro exam + imaging.
    Non-convulsive seizures / status — unexplained ↓LOC → EEG.
    Catatonia — posturing, mutism, waxy flexibility.
    Intoxication / withdrawal — substance history; treat the cause.
    These can coexist with delirium — known dementia does not exclude superimposed delirium.

      B — Both SAT & SBT
      SAT / SBT safety screen & failure criteria

      SAT — do NOT interrupt sedation if: active seizures · alcohol withdrawal on sedation · escalating sedation for agitation · neuromuscular blockade · MI in prior 24 h · raised ICP.

      SAT failure → stop SAT, restart sedation at HALF the prior dose if: sustained anxiety/agitation/pain · RR >35 for >5 min · SpO₂ <88% for >5 min · acute dysrhythmia · ≥2 signs of respiratory distress.

      SBT — proceed only if: SpO₂ >88% on FiO₂ <50% & PEEP <8 · pH >7.15 · no agitation · no ischemia in 24 h · hemodynamically stable · no raised ICP.

      SBT failure → abort, resume support if: RR >35 or <8 · SpO₂ <88% · abrupt mental-status change · dysrhythmia · ≥2 distress signs.

      Loop: pass SAT → proceed to SBT; fail either → restart sedation at half the prior dose, re-screen in 24 h.

      C — Choice of Analgesia & Sedation

      eCASH: minimal sedation is the default — analgesia first, lightest RASS that meets the goal, reassess each shift.

      D — Delirium: Assess, Prevent & Manage
      E — Early Mobility & Exercise

      Safety screen first — reddest parameter wins; a single red = hold & discuss with the team.

      ● Respiratory: FiO₂ <0.6 · SpO₂ >90% · RR <30
      ● Cardiovascular: stable BP, no escalating vasopressors, no active ischemia/arrhythmia
      ● Neuro: RASS-arousable & able to participate
      ● Lines/other: no unstable airway, active bleeding, or unstable fracture

      Progression: passive ROM → active ROM / bed exercises → sit / edge of bed → stand / transfer → ambulate.

        Review each domain each round; document action taken
        0/9 reviewed
        D
        Drugs / Withdrawal

        Deliriogenic agents? Dose reduction? Also withdrawal — alcohol/benzo/opioid (check last use & home meds; in the ICU treat to RASS, not CIWA).

        E
        Eyes / Ears / Env

        Glasses & hearing aids in? Whiteboard updated? Lights on daytime?

        L
        Low O₂ / Liver

        Check SpO₂, Hgb, cardiac/PE events, liver function.

        I
        Infection

        Fever, leukocytosis, cultures pending? Occult sepsis?

        R
        Retention

        Urinary retention or constipation? Bladder scan; disimpact or catheterize if indicated.

        I
        Ictal / Seizure

        Consider non-convulsive seizures / status — esp. with unexplained ↓LOC. Consider EEG.

        U
        Under-hydration / Nutrition

        Volume status, intake, electrolytes. Parenteral thiamine before glucose if malnourished / alcohol use: 100–300 mg IV; 500 mg IV TID if Wernicke suspected.

        M
        Metabolic

        Na, Mg, Ca, glucose, BUN/Cr, acid-base. Pain/retention/constipation?

        (S)
        Subdural / Sleep

        Subdural hematoma (recent fall or anticoagulation)? Sleep deprivation?

            Medications Reference
            Pharmacologic options and deliriogenic agents to review & limit
            Reminder: No agent has FDA approval for delirium treatment. Antipsychotics do not treat or shorten delirium (PADIS: no recommendation for or against) — they control dangerous agitation short-term, then stop. Medications address symptoms only when non-pharmacologic measures fail and safety is at risk.
            Pharmacologic Options — Safety Indication Only
            Drug Typical Dose Notes
            Haloperidol 0.25–0.5mg q4–6h PRN; lowest effective dose, cap per local protocol Elderly more sensitive (EPS/QTc rise with dose); EKG baseline; monitor QTc (caution >500ms); avoid Parkinson's / Lewy body; dementia-related-mortality boxed warning; IV route is off-label (higher QT/Torsades risk) — prefer PO/IM, continuous ECG if given IV. Stop & escalate if NMS (rigidity + hyperthermia + autonomic instability + ↑CK).
            Quetiapine 12.5–25mg q12h PO; lowest dose, shortest duration Sedating; orthostatic hypotension (fall risk); QTc prolongation; antipsychotic-class boxed warning — increased mortality in elderly with dementia-related psychosis.
            Dexmedetomidine 0.2–0.7 mcg/kg/hr IV infusion MV adults: agitation preventing weaning, or when light sedation / delirium reduction is a priority (PADIS 2025, suggested over propofol); monitor bradycardia/hypotension
            Lorazepam Specific Use Per CIWA protocol Rescue / ETOH withdrawal ONLY — may worsen delirium
            Melatonin 0.5–3mg nightly May be considered for sleep / circadian support; low-certainty ICU evidence (PADIS 2025); not a treatment for established delirium, and prevention evidence is mixed
            No antipsychotics at discharge without psychiatric indication.
            Deliriogenic Medications — Review & Limit
            This is a medication-review prompt, not a list of equally harmful drugs. Risk varies by agent, dose, route, renal/hepatic function, drug interactions, and temporal association with the delirium. Some agents (e.g., benzodiazepines, strong anticholinergics, meperidine) are higher-risk; others warrant review only in specific contexts. Beers Criteria apply to adults ≥65.
            Renal/hepatic accumulation: meperidine → normeperidine builds up in renal failure (seizures) — avoid; morphine / hydromorphone metabolites are neurotoxic at GFR <30; gabapentin / pregabalin need ~50% dose reduction at CrCl <60.
            Toggle any medication off to remove it from the printed documents. Changes apply to both the Full Rounding Tool and the SPA Quick Reference.
