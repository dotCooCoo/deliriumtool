Pediatric ICU delirium — risk factors

PICU delirium is common (point prevalence ~25%; higher with mechanical ventilation and developmental delay) and is mostly hypoactive or mixed — easily missed. This is a review aid summarizing reported associations, not a validated predictive score. Minimizing the modifiable factors — benzodiazepines above all — is the actionable lever. Prevention — pediatric ABCDEF / ICU Liberation bundle

Non-pharmacologic, multicomponent prevention is first-line; routine pharmacologic prophylaxis (e.g., scheduled antipsychotics) is not recommended. Honest framing: bundle benefit is best established for mortality and care-process measures, while a direct drop in delirium incidence from bundle adoption alone has been inconsistent — but the individual levers (benzodiazepine minimization above all) are well supported. Treatment approach

Treat the cause first and apply the non-pharmacologic bundle — drugs are adjunctive. Optimize analgesia, switch benzodiazepine sedation to dexmedetomidine, deprescribe anticholinergics, restore sleep, and engage family. Pharmacologic treatment of delirium symptoms is reserved for short-term, refractory agitation or distress that threatens safety when non-pharmacologic measures have failed.

Off-label, limited evidence. No pediatric randomized trial supports antipsychotics for PICU delirium; the pediatric data are retrospective and observational, and a large database study linked antipsychotic use to higher mortality (confounded by indication). Atypicals (risperidone, quetiapine, olanzapine) are generally preferred over IV haloperidol. Use the lowest dose for the shortest time and reassess daily.
Before & during any antipsychotic — monitoring

Baseline 12-lead ECG (QTc), electrolytes (K, Mg, Ca), and review of concurrent QT-prolonging drugs (ondansetron, methadone, macrolides, azoles, certain antiarrhythmics). Act on QTc > 450–500 ms or a ≥ 25% rise from baseline (closer monitoring, dose reduction, or stop). Watch for acute dystonia / EPS (most with haloperidol — treat with IV diphenhydramine or benztropine), akathisia, and NMS (fever, rigidity, autonomic instability, ↑ CK). Over longer courses, watch weight gain and metabolic change (highest with olanzapine and quetiapine).

Sources: SCCM PANDEM 2022 (PMID 35119438); Capino 2020 antipsychotics in pediatric ICU delirium (J Pediatr Pharmacol Ther 2020;25:81, PMC7025750); Madden 2021 antipsychotic prescribing & outcomes (PMID 38571986); QTc-effects study (PMC7792149). Values carry a pediatric-clinician sign-off gate.

Sedation & sleep
Agent Pediatric dose Notes
Dexmedetomidine preferred 0.2–1 mcg/kg/hr IV infusion, titrated to a light goal; a loading bolus is often omitted in the PICU to limit hemodynamic effects. Delirium-sparing — preferred over benzodiazepines. Bradycardia, hypotension (biphasic BP); caution in cardiac disease / AV block. Rebound agitation/hypertension if stopped abruptly after prolonged use — taper. No analgesia or amnesia. Off-label < 18 yr.
Benzodiazepines
(midazolam, lorazepam)		 Limit / avoid as continuous sedation. Independent, dose-related delirium risk. Do not escalate to treat delirium-related agitation. Reserve for specific indications (status epilepticus, sedative / alcohol withdrawal, procedural need).
Melatonin 0.5–3 mg PO at bedtime (younger); up to 3–5 mg (older children / adolescents). Sleep / circadian support — not a delirium treatment. Limited pediatric ICU evidence. Confirm product (immediate- vs extended-release).
Antipsychotics — off-label, limited evidence (short-term, refractory agitation only)
Agent Pediatric dose (enteral unless noted) Notes
Risperidone < 5 yr ~0.1 mg q12–24h; ≥ 5 yr ~0.2 mg q12–24h. ≤ 2 yr ~0.01–0.04 mg/kg/day (mean ~0.02). Titrate; maintenance up to ~2 mg/day. Often first-line oral. QTc (least among atypicals — still monitor), EPS / dystonia, hyperprolactinemia, sedation, metabolic. Baseline + follow-up ECG.
Quetiapine ~0.43–0.7 mg/kg/dose q8h (≈ 1.5 mg/kg/day); reported median ~1.3 mg/kg/day. Sedating (useful at night); often chosen in the cardiac ICU. QTc prolongation reported; orthostasis, metabolic. Baseline + follow-up ECG.
Olanzapine Age-banded start: infants ~0.625 mg; toddlers ~1.25 mg; older / severely agitated ~2.5–5 mg, once–twice daily. ODT useful if swallowing is difficult. Prominent sedation and metabolic / weight gain; anticholinergic effects; QTc.
Haloperidol reserve Reserve for severe agitation when enteral atypicals aren't feasible. IV (off-label): load ~0.025–0.1 mg/kg/dose, may repeat; maintenance ~0.015–0.15 mg/kg/dose IV q6–8h. Oral liquid available. High caution. Dose-dependent QTc prolongation / torsades — continuous ECG, correct K / Mg; IV use carries an FDA torsades warning. More EPS / acute dystonia than atypicals in children (have IV diphenhydramine / benztropine ready). NMS; lowers seizure threshold. Avoid in long-QT, significant hepatic impairment, Parkinsonism.
Deliriogenic medications — review & minimize

Benzodiazepines (strongest modifiable risk) · anticholinergics (diphenhydramine, atropine / glycopyrrolate, scopolamine) · high or escalating opioids (analgesia first, but cumulative dose is a risk) · corticosteroids (dose-related neuropsychiatric effects) · other CNS-acting / GABAergic agents.

Sources: Capino 2020 (J Pediatr Pharmacol Ther 2020;25:81, PMC7025750); Campbell 2020 risperidone ≤ 2 yr (PMID 31771334); Joyce 2015 quetiapine safety (PMID 26469214; median 1.3 mg/kg/day); pediatric ICU delirium systematic review (Pediatr Drugs 2025, PMID 40906237); dexmedetomidine — Phan & Nahata (Pediatr Drugs 2008).
